The 5-Step RFP That Gets Competitive Bids

Most RFPs get poor supplier response because they're too vague. Here's the structure that works.

1 — Scope clarity: Define exactly what you need in plain language.

2 — Evaluation criteria upfront: Tell suppliers how you'll score them. They'll tailor bids accordingly.

3 — Fair timeline: Allow 3–4 weeks. Rushed RFPs attract fewer, weaker bids.

4 — Q&A window: Share all supplier questions with everyone. Reduces clarifications by 60%.

5 — Clear award process: Explain what happens after submission.

Quick-Win Savings You Can Find in 30 Days

Four levers that routinely unlock 5–15% savings before any formal sourcing program.

1. Consolidate suppliers: Consolidating from 6 to 2–3 per category typically delivers 8–12% improvement.

2. Auto-renewing contracts: A renegotiation email at renewal can save 5–10% in days.

3. Payment terms: Net 30 → Net 60–90 improves working capital with zero price impact.

4. Maverick spend: Typically 10–20% of spend is off-contract. Route it back.

Running a Supplier Review Suppliers Respect

A good review drives improvement. A bad one damages the relationship. The framework that works.

Share scorecard first: Send metrics 1 week before — no surprises.

Lead with what's working: Genuine recognition before challenges prevents defensiveness.

Agree on 2–3 actions: Focus on the highest-impact items with clear owners and dates.

End with your pipeline: Suppliers who see upside invest more in the relationship.

7 Contract Clauses Buyers Most Often Miss

Terms rarely in supplier templates but critical for protecting value and managing risk.

1. Most Favored Customer (MFC): Guarantees you the best price offered to anyone.

2. Price index formula: Link changes to CPI, not discretionary increases.

3. Step-in rights: Your right to switch if performance drops.

4. Audit rights: Essential for cost-plus contracts.

5. IP ownership: Custom items belong to you.

6. Change of control: Protects you on acquisition.

7. Termination for convenience: Exit with reasonable notice, no cause needed.

Choosing Procurement Software: Avoid the Traps

Most tech failures aren't about the software — they're about the selection process.

Don't demo first: Define requirements before seeing demos. Demos anchor you to what vendors show.

Evaluate data migration: Ask: "How do we get data in and out?" Poor portability = lock-in.

Check ERP integration: Native connectors only. Custom APIs kill 40% of implementations.

Run a pilot: 4–6 weeks with real spend data before signing.

Category Strategy: The One-Page Framework

A category strategy doesn't need 40 slides. Here's the structure that actually drives decisions.

Snapshot: Total spend, supplier count, YoY trend, top 5 purchases.

Market: Competitive vs consolidated? Price trend? Substitutes?

Kraljic matrix: Strategic / leverage / bottleneck / routine — drives the right approach.

Levers: Consolidation, long-term agreements, demand management, dual sourcing.

Action plan: 3 initiatives, owner, timeline, expected savings.

Advanced

Savings Finance Will Actually Approve

Use this methodology to build credibility with finance and ensure savings get recognized.

Price savings: (Last price – New price) × Actual volume. Not forecasts.

Cost avoidance: (Proposed increase × Volume). Keep the original quote as evidence.

Align with finance early: Agree on methodology before the project, not year-end.

Separate hard/soft: Hard savings hit P&L. Avoidance goes in a separate bucket.

Supplier Onboarding: What to Capture First

The right data upfront prevents compliance headaches and payment delays later.

Legal: Entity name, registration, VAT/tax ID, authorized signatory.

Financial: Bank details, credit references, latest financials for strategic suppliers.

Compliance: Insurance, anti-bribery policy, data protection, modern slavery statement.

ESG: Environmental certifications, diversity status, carbon footprint — increasingly required.
